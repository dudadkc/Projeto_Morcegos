Occurrence and variables

We obtained species occurrence records from multiple sources. We compiled data from Brazilian online databases, including speciesLink (https://specieslink.net), SALVE ICMBio (www.salve.icmbio.gov), SiBBr (https://www.sibbr.gov.br), and the Portal da Biodiversidade (www.sibbr.gov). Additionally, we gathered occurrence records from the literature, including Atlantic Bats (Muylaert et al., 2017), DarkCideS 1.0 (Tanalgo et al., 2022), and vampire bat reports (Van de Vuurst et al., 2022). We also used the spocc R package to retrieve data from the Global Biodiversity Information Facility (GBIF) (http://www.gbif.org), VertNet (https://www.vertnet.org), and iDigBio (https://www.idigbio.org). After downloading and integrating the data, we applied a series of filters to clean the occurrence records. We removed records based on the following criteria: (i) temporal filtering (excluding records before 1970), (ii) low-precision filtering (excluding records with fewer than three decimal places), (iii) spatial filtering based on the Neotropical limit (Marrone et al., 2022) and historical species distributions (Barve et al., 2011), (iv) bias filtering (removing duplicates, invalid reference system coordinates, and records with zero coordinates), and (v) rarefaction at a 50 km resolution. Data cleaning was performed using the CoordinateCleaner (Zizka et al., 2019) and spThin (Aiello-Lammens et al., 2015) R packages. We used four classes of predictor variables: climatic, topographic, hydrographic, and cave-related. The climatic variables (19 bioclimatic variables) were obtained from WorldClim v. 2.1 (Fick & Hijmans, 2017). For topographic variables, we downloaded elevation, slope, and aspect data from EarthEnv (Amatulli et al., 2018; https://www.earthenv.org/topography). We also retrieved hydrographic data from EarthEnv and calculated the Euclidean distance to freshwater ecosystems (Domisch et al., 2015). Finally, for cave-related variables, we calculated the Euclidean distance to karst caves, following Muylaert et al. (2022), using cave data from Chen et al. (2017). All variables were adjusted to the full extent of the Neotropical region at a spatial resolution of 30 arc-seconds (~1 km).

Species distribution modeling 

We employed the maximum entropy machine learning algorithm (MaxEnt) (Phillips et al., 2017) with automated tuning and evaluation (Kass et al., 2021). MaxEnt is one of the most widely used machine learning algorithms for presence-only data and has demonstrated robust performance even with a limited number of occurrences (Phillips et al., 2017). To reduce multicollinearity among predictor variables, we performed a Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) analysis, retaining only variables with VIF < 2 using the usdm R package (Dormann et al., 2013). We generated 10,000 randomly sampled background points for each species and tested different parameter combinations, resulting in a total of 60 models. These models were built by combining 10 regularization multiplier (RM) values (ranging from 0.5 to 5 in increments of 0.5) with six feature class (FC) types: L, LQ, H, LQH, LQHP, and LQHPT, where L = linear, Q = quadratic, H = hinge, P = product, and T = threshold. To assess model performance, we applied a four-fold spatial partitioning block method. Models were fitted and evaluated using the ENMeval 2.0 R package (Kass et al., 2021), which facilitates model tuning, evaluation, and comparison to identify the optimal settings. This tuning process typically results in models that balance goodness-of-fit (i.e., minimizing overfitting) and predictive ability (Hallgren et al., 2019; Radosavljevic & Anderson, 2014). We selected the best model based on the lowest ΔAICc and evaluated model performance using AUC and TSS values under the maximum sensitivity and specificity threshold.
